## Content Summary

China's vocational schools are emerging as a strategic front in supporting domestic businesses' global expansion efforts. As Chinese manufacturers like CATL and BYD establish factories overseas, they face significant challenges in finding workers who understand both Chinese business practices and local market conditions. To address this talent gap, vocational institutions are partnering with companies to create specialized training programs that combine Chinese language instruction with vocational skills in areas such as e-commerce, digital marketing, and information technology.

The initiative represents a shift from traditional education models to industry-aligned training. Schools like the Hebei Software Institute are working with international partners, particularly in Thailand, to design curricula that reflect real-world production environments. Students learn in settings that mirror actual factory floors, live-streaming studios, and logistics hubs, gaining practical experience that prepares them for immediate employment in Chinese-owned overseas facilities. This approach ensures that graduates possess not only technical skills but also cultural fluency that enables them to serve as effective bridges between Chinese corporate culture and local workforces.

By integrating language training with vocational education, China is building a sustainable pipeline of bilingual, skilled workers who can facilitate the smooth operation of Chinese enterprises abroad. The model supports the broader "going out" strategy by addressing one of its most persistent challenges: the shortage of talent capable of navigating both Chinese business methodologies and international market dynamics. As more Chinese companies expand globally, this vocational education approach may become an increasingly important component of China's international economic engagement.
